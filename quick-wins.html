Urgent — losing customers now
01
Reservations don't exist
People are DMing the chef on Instagram trying to book a table. There's no system.
The problem
  • No reservation system anywhere — website, Google, phone
  • Out-of-neighborhood visitors won't risk the drive without a guaranteed table
  • People who read about Ulivo online can't plan a visit
  • Instagram DMs to the chef is not a booking system
  • Parking anxiety keeps suburban visitors from even trying
The fix
  • Two fixed reservation blocks per night: 5:00 PM and 7:00 PM
  • Everything else stays walk-in — preserves the neighborhood energy
  • Reserve 30% of seats for bookings, 70% walk-in
  • Add parking guide to booking confirmation emails
  • Simple system — Resy, OpenTable, or Yelp Reservations

Proposed nightly schedule

Doors open at 3 PM. Two reservation windows. Everything else is walk-in.

3–5
Walk-in only
Early service. Neighborhood regulars, after-work crowd. No reservations needed — keeps it casual.
5:00
Reservation block 1
First seating. Perfect for families, early diners, people coming from outside the neighborhood who want a guaranteed table.
7:00
Reservation block 2
Second seating. Not a full turnover, but early 5pm tables will open up. Prime evening slot for planned visits, groups, date nights.

Capacity recommendation

With ~88 seats (68 inside + 20 patio), we recommend reserving 25–30 seats per block (~30% of capacity). This keeps 70% of the restaurant walk-in, which preserves the neighborhood feel and spontaneous energy that makes Ulivo work.

If reservation demand outpaces supply, that's a good problem — it creates scarcity and urgency. People who can't book will walk in earlier. People who book will feel like they got something special.

Reserved (30%)
Walk-ins (70%)
25–30 seats reserved per block ~60 seats walk-in

Parking guide — include in every booking confirmation

This is the #1 anxiety for out-of-neighborhood visitors. A clear parking guide in the confirmation email removes the excuse not to come.

Before 6:30 PM
Residential permit only
6:30–8:30 PM
2-hr free parking
After 8:30 PM
No enforcement until 9:30 AM
  • 7 PM reservation: Park after 6:30, arrive at 7. You're within the 2-hour window. By 8:30 enforcement ends — stay as long as you want.
  • 5 PM reservation: Street parking is tighter, but spots open as residents leave for the evening. Metered spots on Georgia Ave also available.
  • The signs say "residential parking" — but that means non-residents get 2 hours free. People don't know this. We tell them.
  • After 8:30 PM: No tickets. Your car can sit there overnight until 9:30 AM the next day. Zero risk.
  • Circle the block. People are coming and going constantly. A spot always opens within a few minutes, within a couple blocks.
Urgent — damaging first impressions
02
Google still thinks this is Capitosta
The name changed. Nothing else did. Photos, reviews, and responses are all wrong.
The problem
  • Google Business Profile still shows Capitosta photos
  • Old reviews reference a completely different restaurant
  • Owner photos haven't been updated with Ulivo's actual food
  • Review responses are dismissive and unwelcoming
  • People Googling "Ulivo DC" see a confusing, outdated profile
The fix
  • Upload all professional food photography as owner photos — immediately
  • Write new review responses — warm, welcoming, community-focused
  • Launch review generation campaign to bury old Capitosta content
  • Update business description, hours, menu link, reservation link
  • Add schema markup to website for rich search results

Review response tone — before and after

Current tone — defensive, dismissive
"We're a walk-in only restaurant. If you can't handle that, this probably isn't the place for you."

This pushes away the exact people who want to become regulars. A reservation question is someone saying "I want to come." The answer should never make them feel stupid for asking.

New tone — warm, neighborhood restaurant
"We'd love to have you! We're mostly walk-in and there's almost always room, but we're also rolling out a few reserved time slots at 5 and 7 for folks coming from further out. Can't wait to welcome you in."

Every review response is a public advertisement. Future customers read these before deciding whether to visit. Welcoming tone converts browsers into diners.

Photo strategy

Professional photos from the website already exist. Upload them as owner photos immediately — these get priority placement over user-uploaded images. We can't delete old Capitosta reviews or their attached photos, but we can flood the profile with new, beautiful content that pushes old photos below the fold.

Goal: within 30 days, a Google search for "Ulivo DC" should show Ulivo's food, not Capitosta's.

Important — missing revenue channel
03
Uber Eats doesn't know you exist
The profile isn't set up. No delivery, no pickup, no Uber navigation to the location.
The problem
  • Uber Eats business profile not configured
  • Restaurant doesn't appear in Uber Eats search results
  • No delivery or takeout ordering available
  • Uber drivers can't navigate to correct location
  • Missing an entire revenue channel and discovery platform
The fix
  • Set up Uber Eats merchant profile — menu, photos, hours, location
  • Configure delivery radius and pickup options
  • Set operating hours aligned with kitchen availability
  • Pin correct location for Uber driver navigation
  • Curate a delivery-friendly menu (not everything travels well)

Platform recommendation

Start with Uber Eats for delivery + pickup. It also fixes the Uber navigation issue since drivers will have the correct pin. Consider adding DoorDash in month two once the Uber Eats workflow is dialed in. No need to launch both at once — get one running smoothly first.

Delivery menu should be curated, not the full menu. Pasta travels well. Delicate plating doesn't. We protect the brand by only offering items that arrive looking (and tasting) the way they should.

Important — first impression damage
04
Review responses are pushing people away
Every Google review response is a public ad. Right now, they're advertising attitude instead of hospitality.
Current responses to questions about reservations, parking, and how the restaurant works are coming across as dismissive. People reading these reviews are potential first-time visitors trying to figure out if this place is worth the trip. The tone needs to match the warmth of the actual dining experience.

Respond to every review

Positive or negative. A thoughtful response to a 3-star review can convert the reviewer AND every future reader into a customer.

Thank, don't defend

Even unfair reviews. "Thanks for coming in — we'd love another shot to get it right" beats "well actually" every time.

Answer the real question

"Do you take reservations?" means "I want to come." The answer should make them feel welcome, not corrected.

Mention what's new

Every response is a chance to say "we just launched our spring menu" or "our new pasta is incredible." Subtle marketing in every reply.

Immediate action

Go through every existing Google review response and rewrite the ones that read as dismissive or unwelcoming. This is a one-time cleanup that takes a couple hours but changes how every future visitor perceives the restaurant. We handle this in week one.

Growth — build this quarter
05
Teacher outreach — trust-filtered marketing
Tim is a DC public school parent. That's not just a personal detail — it's a marketing channel.
Teachers are an ideal audience: they're local, they're social, and they eat together. But cold outreach doesn't work. This approach sends the message through the school itself — the trust filter that turns a flyer from junk mail into a recommendation.
01

Design the offer

Half-price pasta for DC public school teachers. Clean, branded postcard that feels like Ulivo — not a coupon.

02

Deliver through schools

Bundle of postcards to the school office with a note from Tim as a parent. School puts one in each teacher's mailbox. Trust filter activated.

03

Build the group booking

Teachers talk. Offer a Teacher Appreciation group dinner — reserve the upstairs (~30 seats) for a school group. Now you've got a private event that fills a slow night.

Why this works

Teachers aren't immediate neighbors but they're in the DC area. They tell each other. They come as groups. They post on social media. One teacher dinner for 20 people generates word-of-mouth that reaches hundreds of parents and colleagues.

The reservation system enables this — a group of 8 teachers can book the 7pm block. The upstairs space enables larger events — 30 seats for a school appreciation night. The EDDM system can deliver the postcards to the school office with a personal note.

This is the kind of campaign that fills a Tuesday night and turns a one-time visit into a regular tradition.
